The rise of the visual and the fall of the public

Where literacy is universal or near-universal, the eye tends to take precedence of the ear. In such a society, especially when education becomes more concerned with science and technology than with the humanities, spelling pronunciations may supersede inherited sounds with considerable rapidity.

The quote above from Simeon Potter’s mid-century Modern Linguistics has been on my mind lately, due in no small part to the explosion of video on the web, and the redirection of publishing resources to it. I wonder what it has to do with the sneaking feeling that political theorists have had for the last half-century on the decline of what we call “the public.”

In particular I wonder because of the apparent trends in traditional non-video publishing (radio and newspaper) to privatize everything. In radio, the trend is to supplant “news” with “storytelling,” to transform events of public interest into personal stories that we experience with intimate, emotional responses. To say nothing of what I like to call the “suffering channel” tendency of storytelling to present human emotional vulnerability as the main attraction. In print sources, this tendency expresses itself in the insertion of first-person plurals into news stories, noted with characteristic acerbity by The Awl.
